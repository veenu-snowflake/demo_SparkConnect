SLIDE 7 - BUILDING ON SPARK CONNECT

Now here is the cool part. We did not invent some proprietary thing. We built on top of something that already exists in the Spark world.

In Spark 3.4, the community introduced something called Spark Connect. It basically splits Spark into a thin client and a server. Your PySpark app is the client. It builds a plan and sends it over to a server for execution.

Usually that server is a Spark cluster. But with Snowpark Connect, Snowflake becomes the server. Your app talks the standard Spark protocol, and Snowflake does all the heavy lifting behind the scenes.

So it is not some hack or workaround. It is built on the open standard.

SLIDE 27 - SPARK CONNECTOR vs SNOWPARK CONNECT

Okay, this is an important distinction. People sometimes confuse these two, so let me be really clear.

The old Spark Connector moves data OUT of Snowflake into your Spark cluster. That means data movement, fragmented governance, extra costs.

Snowpark Connect keeps everything IN Snowflake. Your Spark code runs on the Snowflake engine. No data movement. Unified. Performant. Open.

Think of it this way. Connector means data moves to you. Connect means you move to the data.

SLIDE 30 - HOW IT WORKS UNDER THE HOOD

Let me break it down step by step.

Step one, your client runs standard open source Spark Connect.

Step two, Spark Connect sends the query plan to Snowflake.

Step three, Snowpark Connect interprets that plan.

Step four, it optimizes the plan for Snowflake's engine. Things like query simplification, caching, metadata management.

Step five, it hands the execution off to Snowpark.

Step six, Snowpark runs it on Snowflake's vectorized engine.

So Snowflake is literally speaking the Spark language. You do not have to learn anything new.
